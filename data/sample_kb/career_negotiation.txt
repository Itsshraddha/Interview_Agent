=======================================================================

FOCUS: Salary negotiation, offer evaluation, personal branding, career growth strategies

------------------------------------------------------------------
SECTION 1: SALARY NEGOTIATION
------------------------------------------------------------------

Core principle: Negotiation is expected. 85% of hiring managers have room to negotiate.
The worst outcome is "no" — you will never lose an offer by negotiating professionally.

Pre-negotiation research:
- levels.fyi: real compensation data by company, level, and role (especially for Big Tech).
- Glassdoor / LinkedIn Salary: broader market ranges by title, location, years of experience.
- Blind (anonymous app): candid discussions from employees about comp at specific companies.
- Offer letters from competing companies are your most powerful data point.

Step-by-step negotiation process:
1. Never give a number first if possible: "I'd like to understand the full comp structure
   before discussing specific numbers."
2. If forced to give a range: anchor high. Give a range where your FLOOR is your target.
3. After receiving an offer, pause: "Thank you — this is exciting. Can I have 48 hours to review?"
4. Counter with a specific number, not a range: "Based on my research and the competing offers
   I have, I was hoping for $X. Is there flexibility to get there?"
5. Frame around value and market data, not personal need: never say "I need X to pay my rent."

When they say "this is our best offer":
- "I appreciate that. Is there any flexibility on the signing bonus or equity component?"
- "Could we revisit base salary after my first performance review in 6 months?"
- "Is there any flexibility on the start date or remote work arrangement?"

Negotiating beyond base salary:
- Signing bonus: often easier to increase than base (one-time cost, doesn't compound).
- Equity: negotiate number of shares AND vesting schedule (4 years with 1-year cliff is standard).
- Remote flexibility: worth $5-15k/year in commute cost + quality of life.
- Title: a higher title = higher comp in your NEXT negotiation.
- PTO / vacation days.
- Professional development budget (conferences, courses, books).
- Earlier performance review date (6 months instead of 12).

Tips:
- Competing offers are the single most powerful negotiation leverage you can have.
- Be enthusiastic and collaborative in tone: "I'm very excited about this role. I'd love
  to make this work — can we get to $X?"
- Get all final terms in writing (email confirmation) before resigning your current role.
- Salary negotiation is not adversarial — it is a collaborative process.

SECTION 2: EQUITY & COMPENSATION DEEP DIVE
------------------------------------------------------------------

Q: How do you evaluate an equity offer?
Key concepts:
- Vesting schedule: typically 4 years with a 1-year cliff (nothing for 12 months, then 25%
  vests at month 12, then monthly/quarterly for 3 more years).
- Cliff: vesting doesn't begin until you've stayed for the cliff period.
- Strike price (exercise price): price at which you can buy your options. Lower = better.
- 409A valuation: independent appraisal of the company's fair market value (for tax purposes).
  Your strike price ≈ 409A FMV at time of grant.
- ISOs (Incentive Stock Options): favorable tax treatment for employees. Subject to AMT.
  Must exercise within 90 days of leaving — plan accordingly.
- NSOs (Non-qualified Stock Options): taxed as ordinary income at exercise regardless of
  whether you sell. Less favorable than ISOs.
- RSUs (Restricted Stock Units): actual shares granted on a vesting schedule. No strike price.
  Taxed as ordinary income at vesting. Standard at public companies (Google, Amazon, Meta).
- Common vs. Preferred: startups issue common stock to employees, preferred to investors.
  In a downside exit, preferred shareholders are paid first — your common stock may be worthless.

Evaluating startup equity:
1. Ask for the total shares outstanding (fully diluted cap table).
2. Calculate your ownership percentage: your_shares / total_shares_outstanding.
3. Estimate current value: ownership % × 409A valuation.
4. Model exit scenarios: conservative (1x revenue), base (3x), optimistic (10x).
5. Factor in dilution from future funding rounds (typically 20-30% per round).
6. Consider liquidation preferences: if investors have 2x participating liquidation preference,
   they get paid before common shareholders in a modest exit.

Tips:
- RSUs at a public company (liquid, known value) > options at an uncertain-exit startup.
- Ask: "What is the last 409A valuation?" and "What % of the company do these shares represent?"
- Time to liquidity matters: options expiring in 5 years at a startup still in Series B are risky.

SECTION 3: OFFER EVALUATION FRAMEWORK
------------------------------------------------------------------

Evaluate an offer holistically using this scorecard (rate each 1-10, weight by personal priority):

Financial:
- Base salary (vs. market data from levels.fyi and competing offers)
- Total comp (base + bonus + equity annual value)
- Equity quality (RSUs at public co vs. options at startup, strike price, vesting)
- Bonus structure (guaranteed vs. discretionary; target % vs. actual historical payout)
- Benefits: health insurance quality, 401k match %, dental/vision coverage

Career growth:
- Learning velocity: will you be challenged and acquire new skills?
- Promotion frequency and clarity: is the promotion criteria transparent?
- Brand value: does this company name on your resume open doors in 3-5 years?
- Management quality: research your potential manager on LinkedIn and Glassdoor.

Culture & life quality:
- Remote/hybrid flexibility
- Team culture (Glassdoor reviews, informational interviews with current employees)
- Mission alignment: do you care about what the company does?
- Tech stack: will you work with modern, marketable technologies?

Decision framework:
- Write down your must-haves (dealbreakers) vs. nice-to-haves before evaluating any offer.
- If evaluating multiple offers: don't rush — most companies will give you 3-5 business days.
- When comparing offers: use total comp over 4 years (include equity vesting schedule).

SECTION 4: PERSONAL BRANDING & ONLINE PRESENCE
------------------------------------------------------------------

LinkedIn profile optimization:
- Headline: [Role] | [2-3 key skills/technologies] | [Differentiator]
  Example: "Backend Engineer | Python, Kubernetes, AWS | Building distributed systems at scale"
- Summary (About section): 3-4 sentences. Lead with your value proposition, not your job history.
  Include: your expertise, the problems you solve, what you're passionate about, call to action.
- Experience: each role should have 3-5 bullet points with quantified achievements.
  Format: [Action verb] [what you did] → [quantified result]. Example:
  "Redesigned the checkout API → reduced latency by 40%, saving $200K/year in infrastructure cost."
- Skills: prioritize skills that appear in job descriptions for your target roles.
- Recommendations: 3+ thoughtful recommendations from managers/senior peers are high-signal.

GitHub portfolio tips:
- Pin 4-6 repositories that represent your best and most relevant work.
- Every pinned repo must have: a detailed README with screenshots/demo link, clear setup instructions,
  and documentation of key design decisions.
- Contribution graph matters: consistent green squares signal active engagement.
- Contribute to 1-2 open source projects — shows collaboration skills and real-world code quality.

Personal website / portfolio:
- Must-haves: projects section with problem → solution → tech stack → outcome, About page,
  resume download, contact/LinkedIn link.
- Showcase: live demos of projects (Vercel, Netlify, Render — all free tier) carry more weight than GitHub links.
- For ML/Data roles: include a Kaggle profile or published notebooks.
- Domain: your-name.dev or your-name.com — invest $10/year in a personal domain.

SECTION 5: "TELL ME ABOUT YOURSELF" TEMPLATES
------------------------------------------------------------------

Structure: Present → Past → Future (always in this order).

Entry Level template:
"I recently graduated with a [degree] in [field] from [university], where I focused on
[specialization]. During my studies, I [key project/internship that demonstrates the core skill].
Before that, I [relevant experience or achievement]. I'm excited about this [role] at [company]
because [specific reason tied to company mission/tech stack] — I'd love to contribute my [skill]
to [specific team/product goal]."

Mid Level template:
"I'm a [role] with [X] years of experience specializing in [2-3 core areas]. Most recently at
[company], I [key achievement with quantified impact]. Before that at [previous company], I [relevant
achievement]. I'm looking to [next step — more scope, different domain, leadership] and [company]
stood out because [specific reason: tech stack, scale, mission, team]."

Senior Level template:
"I've spent the last [X] years building and leading [type of systems/teams] at [company/companies].
My most recent work at [company] involved [high-impact achievement at scale]. I'm known for [2-3
defining strengths — e.g., simplifying complex systems, building high-performing teams, shipping
at scale]. I'm exploring opportunities where I can [specific goal], and [company]'s work on [specific
product/challenge] is exactly the type of problem I want to tackle next."

Tips:
- Practice your "Tell me about yourself" until it's completely natural — it sets the tone.
- Tailor it to the company: reference something specific about them every time.
- Keep it under 90 seconds — it's an intro, not a monologue.

SECTION 6: INTERVIEW POST-MORTEM TEMPLATE
------------------------------------------------------------------

After every interview round, document the following:
1. Questions asked (exact wording if possible)
2. Your answer — what went well, what felt weak
3. Interviewer's reaction and follow-up questions
4. What you wish you had said differently
5. Topics you didn't know — add to study list
6. General vibe: culture fit signals, team energy, red/green flags
7. Your interest level in the role (1-10) after the interview — gut check

Using post-mortems:
- Review before the next round with the same company.
- Build a personal Q&A library from repeated questions across companies.
- Track which question categories you consistently struggle with — focus prep there.
- Share anonymized notes with peers for mutual benefit.
